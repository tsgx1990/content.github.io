# 50+ AI Story Prompts for Fiction Writers (Copy-Paste, by Stage)

Most prompt lists hand you one-off examples like "write a story about a haunted lighthouse." Those are fun for five minutes and useless by Tuesday, because you can't reuse them on your own book.

This is a different kind of list. Every prompt below is a reusable template with `[bracketed slots]` you fill with your story's specifics, and they're grouped by the stage of writing you're actually in: finding the idea, building the plot, developing characters, worldbuilding, drafting scenes, writing dialogue, and revising. Paste them into ChatGPT, Claude, or a fiction tool like Sudowrite or NovelCrafter. They're tool-agnostic on purpose, and they're free, which the bigger curated prompt libraries usually aren't.

One rule before you start. These are prompts you give an AI as a collaborator, not a "write my novel" button. You stay the author. The prompts that work treat the model as a sharp brainstorming partner who reacts to your specifics, which is why almost every one asks the AI to build on something you provide. ## How to make any of these prompts work better

Three habits turn a generic answer into a useful one:

- **Feed it context.** Paste your premise, character profile, or the last scene before you ask. A prompt with no context gets you the model's average.
- **Reject the first answer once.** The second attempt is almost always less safe. "That's too predictable, give me three riskier versions" raises the floor every time.
- **Add a constraint.** Specifics force originality. "Set it in a flooded subway" beats "set it somewhere interesting."

## Idea and premise prompts

Use these when you have a spark but no shape yet. Push them by stacking two unrelated ideas together.

1. > Give me 10 one-sentence premises for a [genre] story that combine [element A] with [element B]. Make each one a clear situation with built-in conflict, not just a setting.
2. > I have this rough idea: [describe it]. Ask me five questions that would help me find the actual story inside it. One at a time.
3. > Take this premise: [premise]. Generate five "what if" twists that raise the stakes or flip the reader's assumption.
4. > Suggest five protagonists who would find [situation] the hardest possible person to face. Explain the contradiction in each.
5. > Give me three story premises where the antagonist is right and the hero is wrong. Keep them morally uncomfortable.
6. > I want to write about [theme] without being preachy. Suggest five concrete situations that would dramatize it through action instead of dialogue.
7. > Here are three premises I'm considering: [list]. Pressure-test each one. Which can carry a full novel, which is really a short story, and why?

## Plot and structure prompts

Turn a premise into a shape. The "flag anything where nothing changes" instruction is the one that catches filler early.

8. > Based on this premise [premise], draft a chapter-by-chapter outline for a [N]-chapter [genre] novel. For each chapter give the goal, the POV character, and what changes by the end. Flag any chapter where nothing changes.
9. > Here's my outline: [paste]. Where does the middle sag? Point to the exact chapters where tension drops and suggest what could raise it.
10. > Give me five possible inciting incidents for [premise], ranked from quietest to most explosive. Note the tone each one sets.
11. > My story needs a midpoint reversal. Here's the setup: [paste]. Suggest three turns that change what the protagonist wants, not just what happens to them.
12. > List five subplots that would deepen the main plot of [premise] by echoing or contrasting its theme. Keep them tied to existing characters.
13. > I want to plant a twist that's surprising but fair. Given this ending [describe], suggest three clues I can seed earlier without giving it away.
14. > Map this premise onto three different story structures (three-act, hero's journey, and a non-linear option). Show me the beats for each so I can pick.
15. > Here's my climax [describe]. Work backward and tell me what has to be true earlier for this to land emotionally.

## Character prompts

The model's default character is the brave, haunted orphan. Name what you don't want and it complies. For the full method, see {% post_link how-to-develop-fictional-characters-with-ai "how to develop fictional characters with AI" %}.

16. > Interview me about my character [name], a [one-line description]. Ask eight sharp questions about wants, fears, contradictions, and what they're lying to themselves about. One at a time.
17. > Based on what I've told you, give this character one core contradiction and one flaw that's a strength taken too far. Avoid the haunted-orphan and chosen-one templates.
18. > Write 150 words of [character] speaking in first person about [topic]. No stage directions, just voice. Then rewrite it as they'd say it to someone they're trying to impress.
19. > Give my character three specific, unglamorous habits that reveal who they are. "Re-uses tea bags and lies about it" beats "is frugal."
20. > Here are my protagonist and antagonist [describe both]. What do they have in common that makes their conflict personal rather than generic?
21. > Suggest five ways [character]'s greatest strength could cause the worst problem in the plot.
22. > Compress everything I've told you about [character] into a reusable profile under 150 words: essence, want, wound, contradiction, flaw, voice notes, three speech tics.

## Worldbuilding prompts

Build a world that creates conflict, not just scenery. The trick is to ask for rules with costs.

23. > For a [genre] story, invent five rules for [the magic system / the technology / the society] where each rule has a built-in cost or limit that could drive conflict.
24. > Describe [location] through the eyes of [character] at [time of day]. Filter every detail through their current emotion, no neutral description.
25. > My world has this rule: [rule]. Walk through five everyday consequences ordinary people would live with because of it.
26. > Invent a piece of history for [place] that still causes tension in the present day of my story. Tie it to a current character if you can.
27. > Give me five sensory details (smell, sound, texture) that would make [setting] feel lived-in rather than generic.
28. > Design a faction or institution in [world] with a sympathetic goal and an ugly method. Explain how my protagonist would collide with it.
29. > Here's my world so far: [paste]. Point out three things that don't make internal sense or contradict each other.

## Scene drafting prompts

Draft scene by scene, never "the next chapter." Always paste a voice sample so it sounds like you.

30. > Here's my character profile [paste] and this scene goal: [one line]. Write the scene in third-person limited from [character]'s POV, about 800 words, matching this voice sample: [paste 150 words of your prose]. End on the moment of decision, not after it.
31. > Write the same scene three ways: tense and fast, slow and dread-filled, and darkly funny. I'll pick the tone.
32. > Here's a flat scene I wrote: [paste]. Don't rewrite it. Tell me where the tension drops and what's missing, so I can fix it myself.
33. > Give me five ways to open this scene [describe] in the middle of action instead of with setup.
34. > My character needs to get from [point A] to [point B] emotionally in one scene. Suggest the turning beat that earns the shift.
35. > Rewrite this paragraph [paste] using "show don't tell." Keep my voice and don't add new plot.
36. > Suggest three small physical actions [character] could do during this conversation to show what they're feeling without naming the emotion.

## Dialogue prompts

Dialogue is where characters stop sounding like the same narrator. Test it by hiding the names.

37. > Here are two characters [describe both]. Write a tense exchange where they want opposite things but neither says it directly. Subtext only.
38. > Take this on-the-nose dialogue [paste] and rewrite it so the characters talk around the real issue.
39. > Give [character] a distinct verbal tic or speech pattern based on this profile [paste], then write three lines that show it.
40. > Write the same line of dialogue [line] as five different characters would say it, so I can hear the range.
41. > Here's a dialogue scene [paste]. Strip the dialogue tags and tell me if I can still tell who's speaking. If not, where does it blur?
42. > My characters need to deliver this information [describe] without it sounding like exposition. Suggest three ways to bury it in conflict.

## Revision and editing prompts

Use AI as a tough first reader, not a rewriter. The "don't rewrite it" instruction protects your voice.

43. > Read this chapter as a critical reader. Don't rewrite it. Tell me where tension drops, which paragraph is weakest, and what question the reader is left with.
44. > Check this chapter [paste] against these established facts [paste] and flag any continuity contradictions. Use a severity label for each.
45. > Here's my opening page [paste]. Would an agent keep reading? Be blunt about exactly where attention would drop.
46. > Find my crutch words and repeated sentence openings in this passage [paste]. List them with counts. Don't fix them, just show me.
47. > This scene runs long. Tell me which three paragraphs I could cut with the least loss, and what each one was doing.
48. > Read this dialogue-heavy scene [paste] and tell me where I need a beat of action or interiority to break it up.
49. > Here's my chapter ending [paste]. Does it pull the reader forward? Suggest three sharper final lines without changing the events.
50. > Act like a structural editor. Here's my synopsis [paste]. Diagnose the single biggest structural weakness and give me the minimal fix, not a full rewrite.
51. > Scan this passage [paste] for places where I tell an emotion instead of showing it. Quote each line and explain.
52. > Here's feedback I got from a reader [paste]. Help me see the real problem behind the symptom they described, then suggest options.

## How to keep these prompts from sounding generic

A prompt is only half the work. The output still needs you, or it reads like every other AI story. Three quick guardrails:

- **Always paste a voice sample** in any drafting prompt. The model imitates what it sees, so give it your prose, not a blank slate.
- **Do an edit pass on everything.** Cut the throat-clearing first sentence, replace any line that could belong to any character, and fix the rhythm. The unedited output is what gives AI fiction its flat, sourceless feel.
- **Throw away the brainstorm once it's done its job.** Read the ten premises, then close the window and write your own. The list was a spark, not a draft.

## FAQ

### What are the best AI prompts for writing a story?

The best prompts are reusable templates you fill with your own specifics, organized by the stage you're in: premise, plot, character, worldbuilding, scene, dialogue, and revision. The 52 above are built that way. Generic one-off prompts ("write a story about X") are far less useful than a template you can run on your actual book.

### How do I prompt ChatGPT to write a story?

Give it context first (premise, character profile, tone), assign it a role ("act as a story editor"), add a constraint, and ask for one piece at a time rather than a whole book. Paste a sample of your own writing as a voice reference. The drafting and scene prompts above follow this structure.

### Are AI story prompts cheating?

Using AI to brainstorm, outline, and pressure-test your own ideas is no more cheating than workshopping with a writing group. It becomes a problem when you publish raw generated output as your own work. Used as the collaborator these prompts assume, you stay the author.

### Which AI is best for fiction writing prompts?

Any strong chat model (ChatGPT, Claude) handles these well. For drafting across a long manuscript where consistency matters, a fiction-native tool that stores your characters and world beats a raw chat window. See our tools guides for the trade-offs.

### How do I stop AI writing from sounding generic?

Paste a voice sample, reject the first safe answer, add specific constraints, and always do a human edit pass. Generic input and zero editing produce generic output. The guardrails section above covers it.
