# How to Overcome Writer's Block with AI (and When Not To)

Most advice on this either oversells AI as a magic cure or warns you off it entirely. Both are wrong. AI can absolutely get you unstuck, but used lazily it deepens the block, because outsourcing the hard thinking is exactly how you lose momentum *and* your voice.

The trick is to stop treating "writer's block" as one problem. It's at least four, and each one needs a different AI move. Below is how to diagnose which block you have, the specific prompt that breaks it, and the one rule that keeps the words still yours.

## First, the honest part: AI can make a block worse

If your block is fear (of the blank page, of it not being good enough), then having AI write the paragraph for you doesn't cure the fear. It teaches you that the way through fear is to hand the work to a machine. Do that enough and you can't start anything without it.

So the goal is never "AI writes the stuck part." The goal is "AI gets me moving again, then I take over." Keep that distinction and AI is a jump-start. Lose it and it becomes a crutch.

## Type the block, then pick the AI move

Diagnose before you prompt. Which of these is actually happening?

### Blank page: generate 10 *bad* openings to react against

A blank page is paralysing because every option feels equally weighty. Remove the weight by asking for deliberately mediocre options, since it's far easier to react than to create:

> Give me 10 deliberately mediocre, clichéd opening lines for a scene where [situation]. Make them obvious and overdone — I want to react against them, not use them.

You'll read them, feel "ugh, not that," and the version you actually want will surface by contrast. You're not using AI's words; you're using your own reaction to them.

### The sagging middle: interrogate your characters' wants

A middle sags when the plot is moving but nobody *wants* anything urgently. Don't ask AI for plot twists. Ask it to pressure your characters:

> My story has stalled in the middle. Ask me, one at a time, what each main character wants RIGHT NOW and what's stopping them. Push me on any answer that's vague.

The unstick usually comes from your own answer to "what does she want right now," not from the AI. (If the characters themselves feel thin, that's a different fix; see {% post_link how-to-develop-fictional-characters-with-ai "how to develop fictional characters with AI" %}.)

### Fear and perfectionism: dictate, let AI clean up later

If you freeze because the first sentence isn't good enough, the problem is self-editing while drafting. Separate the two jobs: get the raw material out badly, then fix it later.

> I'm going to dump messy, unstructured thoughts about this scene. Don't rewrite them yet — just ask me three questions that will help me get the rest of the scene out of my head.

The point is to keep *you* generating. AI tidying comes after the draft exists, never during.

### Lost the thread: have AI summarise what you've written

Sometimes you're stuck because you've forgotten where the story was going. Paste in your last chapter or two and ask:

> Summarise what's happened in this draft so far, then list the 3 open questions a reader would most want answered next.

Those open questions are almost always your next scene. AI didn't invent the direction. It reflected back the one you'd already set.

## The one rule: AI on the scaffolding, never the voice

Here's the line that keeps the work yours: **use AI on the scaffolding (outlines, questions, summaries, options to react against), but write the actual prose yourself.**

A useful habit: whatever AI generates to unstick you, *throw it away* once you're moving. Read the ten bad openings, then close the window and write your own. The output was a spark, not a draft. Voice is the one thing you can't outsource without it stopping being yours, and increasingly, readers and editors can tell.

## When *not* to reach for AI

- **When you haven't tried the page yet.** If AI is your first move every session, you're training dependency. Sit with the blank page for ten minutes first.
- **When the block is telling you something.** Sometimes you're stuck because the scene is wrong: the character wouldn't do this, or the plot took a false turn. AI will happily generate past a problem you should *listen* to. A walk beats a prompt here.
- **When you're tired, not blocked.** Fatigue masquerades as block. The fix is rest, not a tool.

## FAQ

### What are the best AI prompts for writer's block?

Match the prompt to the block: ask for 10 *bad* openings to beat a blank page, have AI interrogate your characters' wants for a sagging middle, ask it for three questions (not a rewrite) when perfectionism freezes you, and have it summarise your draft plus list open questions when you've lost the thread. The full prompts are in the sections above.

### Is AI a cure for writer's block?

No, and the pieces saying so are right to be skeptical. AI is a jump-start, not a cure. It's excellent at removing the blank-page paralysis and reflecting your story back to you, but if you use it to write the stuck part *for* you, it deepens the dependency that causes block in the first place.

### Can I use AI for writer's block without losing my voice?

Yes, if you keep AI on the scaffolding (outlines, questions, options, summaries) and write the prose yourself. The safest habit is to discard whatever AI generated once it has unstuck you, then write your own version from the spark.

### Which AI is best for getting unstuck on a novel?

Any strong chat model (ChatGPT, Claude) works for the prompts above. For sustained drafting once you're moving, a fiction-aware tool with a story bible beats a raw chat window. See the linked guide.

## Bottom line

Writer's block isn't one problem, so there's no one fix. Diagnose it first: blank page, sagging middle, fear, or lost thread. Then use AI to remove the paralysis (bad options to react against, questions that pressure your story, a summary that finds your next beat) and take over the moment you're moving. Keep AI on the scaffolding, keep the prose yours, and know when the block is signal rather than noise. Used that way, AI doesn't replace your writing. It gets you back to it.
