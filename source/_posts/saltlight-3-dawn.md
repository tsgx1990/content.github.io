# Chapter 3 — Dawn

Mara waited through the last of the dark with a wrecking-bar across her knees.

She had carried it up from the boat-store before midnight and sat with it in the lamp room while
the beam went round and round, dutiful, walking the empty water, asking. There was no boat to
answer it tonight. The sea had been paid. But there would be another season, and another boat, and
another name, for as long as the light kept its bargain — and the light would keep its bargain
forever, because keepers kept the light. That was the whole machine. Her mother had been a tooth in
it. So had she.

The ninth pane hummed as the beam swept past, low in her teeth, the note of a thing remembering a
name. *Edan.* She held the memory of him the way you hold a coal, because letting go of it felt
like the last cruelty. The rope-burn scar. The plain hazel eyes full of borrowed light. *It's
calling me by name,* he had said, and gone, and the island had bought a year with him.

R1 was the first rule and the last. *The lamp burns dusk to dawn. If it fails before dawn, the
tide keeps what it's taken.* She had been raised to hear that as a threat. She understood it now
as a price, and a release. The tide would keep Edan; it would have kept him regardless; nothing she
broke tonight could give him back. But a light that failed could not call. A lamp gone dark asked
for no more names.

Grey was coming into the east, the thin line that meant the night was nearly survived. Almost
dawn. If she waited the few more minutes, the lamp would make it to morning honest, and the
bargain would hold one more clean night, and she could tell herself she had kept faith.

She did not wait.

She stood, and set the flat of the bar against the cracked ninth pane, and put forty years of split
and salt-cracked hands behind it. The glass had been waiting to break since before she was born. It
went all at once, corner to corner along the old hairline and then everywhere, a bright hard rain
of it across the lamp-room floor, and the humming stopped — not satisfied this time, the way it had
stopped for Edan, but simply *ended,* a held note cut off. The lens, blinded in its ninth eye, threw
the flame wrong, scattered it, and the great walking light came apart into ordinary lamplight that
reached no further than the rocks.

For the first time since her mother's mother, the Saltlight did not call.

It was not yet dawn. She had let it fail. She stood in the broken glass and waited for the tide to
come up the path and take the island down into the dark, because that was the other half of the
threat, the part no keeper had ever dared test.

The tide did not come. The sea lay flat and grey and ordinary under the rising light, paying her no
mind at all, the way the sea pays no mind to anything once you have stopped feeding it. The island
held. The water was only water.

When the sun cleared the horizon Mara was still standing there, an old woman with empty hands among
the wreckage of a light, alive on a shore that no longer drowned anyone, and no longer saved anyone
either. She swept up the glass. There was no one to call, and no one left to come, and for the
first time in forty years the dawn asked nothing of her at all.
