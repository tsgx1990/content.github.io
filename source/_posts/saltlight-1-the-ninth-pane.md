# Chapter 1 — The Ninth Pane

Mara climbed the lamp at dusk, the way she had climbed it every dusk for forty years, and the
stairs gave back the same iron complaints they always had. Her hands found the rail without
looking. They were salt-cracked at every knuckle, split and healed and split again, a keeper's
hands. Her mother's had looked the same at the end.

The lamp room smelled of oil and cold glass. She trimmed the wick, set the flame, and watched the
great lens take the small fire and throw it out across the water as something vast. Light walked
the sea. That was the whole of the work: take a little flame and make it large enough for the
water to notice.

There were nine panes in the lens housing, and eight of them were honest. The ninth was not. It
had cracked in a winter storm before Mara was born, a hairline running corner to corner, and her
mother had never replaced it because, she said, the light liked it. When the beam swept round and
touched the ninth pane, the glass hummed — a low note Mara felt in her teeth more than heard. She
had stopped being afraid of it as a girl. Tonight, for no reason she could name, the fear came
back, small and cold, like water finding a seam in a boot.

She told herself it was the weather. The glass said dusk to dawn, her mother had told her, the
first rule and the last: *the lamp burns dusk to dawn, and you do not let it fail, whatever you see
out there, whatever you think you hear.* Mara had asked, once, what happened if it failed. Her
mother had looked at her for a long time and said only, *the tide keeps what it's owed.* Then she
had gone down the stairs and not explained, the way the island never explained anything it was
afraid of.

The beam came round. The ninth pane hummed.

Out past the reef, where no honest boat should be at this hour, a sail showed against the failing
sky. Mara stood very still. Boats did not come here; the charts marked the island with the small
crossed lines that meant *nothing for you, stay away.* And yet the sail held its line, and the
line it held was not away from the light but toward it, straight in, as though the helmsman had
seen the lamp and decided it was a thing to be reached rather than feared.

"No," Mara said, to no one. "Not here. Go round."

The sail did not go round. The lamp turned, and the ninth pane hummed, and the small boat came on
toward the reef with the terrible patience of something being drawn. Mara's hand was on the lens
housing before she knew it had moved, her palm flat to the cold glass, as if she could turn the
beam aside with the weight of her own arm. She could not. The light went where the lens sent it,
out and out, walking the water, and the water, tonight, was answering.

She watched the boat strike. She heard, a long moment later, the sound of it coming apart on the
reef — a sound like a door breaking — and then the dark took the place where the sail had been.

The lamp burned. It was not yet dawn. She did not let it fail.

But she went down the stairs faster than her knees wanted, out into the wind, down the path to the
cove where the tide brought everything in the end, because some part of her already knew the sea
had not finished, that the light had called and the water meant to answer, and she did not yet
understand what it would cost her to be standing there when it did.
