# Chapter 2 — Salt

She found him at the tide-line, face down in the wrack, and for a long moment she was certain he
was another drowned thing the sea had spat back. Then he coughed, and brine ran out of him, and he
was alive.

He was young, younger than her own children would have been. Copper hair, dark with water and
plastered to his skull. When she turned his hands to chafe the warmth back into them she found an
old rope-burn scar across the left palm, white and smooth, the mark of a line run too fast through
a grip. A sailor's scar. He had worked boats his whole short life, and a boat had brought him here,
to the one shore the charts told sailors to fear.

"You shouldn't have come in," she said, because it was true and because she did not know what else
to say.

"Saw the light." His voice was a wreck of salt. He looked up at her with the dazed gratitude of a
man who believes a light means rescue. "Steered for it. You — you saved my —"

"No." She did not let him finish. "I didn't."

She got him up the path and into the keeper's house and put the fire to him, and while the kettle
ticked she sat with her salt-cracked hands open on her knees and made herself understand the thing
she had spent forty years not understanding. The lamp did not warn boats off. It never had. It
called them in, the way it had called this one, steered a tired man straight onto the reef — and
then the sea came for what the light had promised it. *What the light calls, the sea returns,* her
mother had not quite said, *but it asks a name in return.* That was the trade. The island did not
drown because the lamp kept it safe. The island stayed because, every season, the lamp gave the
tide a name to keep.

"What's your name?" she asked him, and was afraid of the question in a way she had never been
afraid of anything.

"Edan," he said.

Above them, faintly, through the floor and the stairs and forty years of habit, she heard the
ninth pane begin to hum.

He heard it too. She watched it take him — watched the dazed gratitude go out of his face and
something else come into it, calm and certain and wrong, the same terrible patience the sail had
held coming in. He stood up from the fire as though someone had called his name across a room.

"Edan," she said. "Sit down. Edan, look at me."

He looked at her. His grey — no. His eyes were not grey; hers were grey; his were a plain seawater
hazel, and they were full of the light. "It's all right," he told her, gently, the way you
reassure someone else's grief. "It's calling me by name."

She put herself in the doorway. She was an old woman with split hands and he was a young man the
sea had decided on, and it was no contest at all. He went past her into the dark and down toward
the cove, unhurried, and she followed shouting his name as though her voice could be louder than
the lamp, and it could not. The water was very black and very calm. He walked into it the way
other men walk into sleep, and the tide closed over the copper of his hair, and the humming
stopped, satisfied, and Saltlight had paid for another year.

The lamp burned behind her on the hill. It was not yet dawn. She had not let it fail.

For the first time in her life, standing in the surf with her empty hands, she understood that
this was the failure — that keeping the light was the cost, and had always been the cost, and that
her mother had known, and her mother's mother, all of them keepers of a thing that ate the people
it pretended to save.

When dawn came she climbed the stairs. She did not trim the wick. She looked at the ninth pane,
cracked corner to corner, humming faintly even now with the memory of a name, and she began to
understand what a keeper would have to break to end it.
